## The one-paragraph summary

A-FINE — short for **Adaptive Fidelity-Naturalness Evaluator** — is an image-quality metric. You feed it two images, a reference and a distorted version of the reference, and it scores how good the distorted one is on a scale that correlates with human judgement. What makes A-FINE different from older metrics like PSNR or SSIM is that it does not assume the reference image is itself perfect. When the reference is also low-quality, A-FINE quietly down-weights the "how faithful is the distorted image to this reference" signal and gives more weight to "how natural does the distorted image look on its own". That adaptive blend is where the design intelligence lives, and shipping it into Burn meant porting a CLIP-based Vision Transformer, two assessment heads, a five-parameter calibrator, and an adapter into the Rust framework without depending on anything outside of it.

## What it is, in plain terms

Imagine you have two photos of the same cat. One is the original. The other has been compressed too hard for WhatsApp and looks a bit smudgy. How much worse is the second one?

You could measure the per-pixel difference and report a number. That is essentially what **PSNR** does, and it has a problem: shift the second photo one pixel to the right and the per-pixel difference explodes, even though a human would say nothing meaningful changed. PSNR gives you the wrong answer there.

You could measure structural similarity — how the local patches of the image line up — and report a number. That is **SSIM**, and it is better than PSNR for most common distortions, but it still falls down on things like blur (it over-penalises) and on situations where the reference image is *itself* not all that good (it has no opinion about that — it just measures similarity).

A-FINE is the next step out. It runs both images through a CLIP Vision Transformer (a neural network pre-trained to understand image content), pulls out feature representations from a middle layer, and then asks two separate questions:

1. **Fidelity** — how close are the two feature representations to each other?
2. **Naturalness** — how plausible does each image look on its own, independent of the other?

Then it blends those two answers together. The blend is the *adaptive* part: when the reference image looks high-quality (high naturalness), fidelity carries most of the score. When the reference image is itself bad, naturalness of the distorted image starts to matter more. That asymmetric blend is what the paper introduces, and it lets A-FINE handle modern situations — generative enhancement methods that produce outputs *better* than the reference, super-resolution methods running on already-blurry inputs — that classical full-reference metrics get wrong.

For Burn, the relevant fact is that A-FINE is a well-defined, reproducible neural metric with public weights — exactly the shape that fits next to LPIPS, DISTS, and FID in the framework's `burn-train::metric::vision` module.

## The strategy call

The decision that mattered most was the one we settled on 2026-04-23: **single PR, inline the CLIP ViT, follow LPIPS's pattern**.

The alternative — a backbone-first PR that adds a reusable CLIP-ViT module, then a follow-up PR that adds A-FINE on top of it — would have been more "correct" in an abstract software-engineering sense. Future CLIP-based metrics could share the backbone. The PR would be smaller and easier to review. The architectural separation would be cleaner.

We rejected it for three reasons:

| Reason | Why |
|---|---|
| Precedent | LPIPS, DISTS, FID all ship as single-PR inlined-backbone implementations. The reviewers know how to read that shape. A novel two-PR pattern would introduce friction without enough payoff. |
| Reusability is speculative | At the moment of the PR, A-FINE was the only metric that needed a CLIP ViT. Building a reusable component on n=1 is a textbook over-abstraction risk. |
| Cost asymmetry | Inlining now and extracting later when a second CLIP-based metric arrives is cheap. Building the reusable abstraction now and discovering it doesn't fit the second metric's needs is expensive. |

There is a CLAUDE-Code-style rule worth naming here: three similar lines is better than a premature abstraction. The Rust ML ecosystem in 2026 doesn't yet have the second CLIP-based metric in Burn. When it lands, the refactor extracts a `ClipViTBackbone` module that both A-FINE and the new metric depend on. Until then, the inlined CLIP-ViT lives entirely inside the `afine/` subfolder and the cost of having it there is zero.

## The fidelity formula

The fidelity head computes an SSIM-style ratio, but in CLIP feature space rather than pixel space. The intuition is the same as SSIM's: a good similarity measure considers means, variances, and covariance jointly so that constant offsets and pure-magnitude changes don't dominate. The formula is roughly:

```
                 (2 · μ_d · μ_r + c1) · (2 · σ_dr + c2)
  fidelity = ───────────────────────────────────────────
              (μ_d² + μ_r² + c1) · (σ_d² + σ_r² + c2)
```

Where `μ_d` and `μ_r` are the per-channel means of the distorted and reference patch features, `σ_d²` and `σ_r²` are the variances, `σ_dr` is the covariance, and `c1`, `c2` are small stabilising constants that prevent the ratio from blowing up when the denominators get close to zero.

Those stabilising constants are where things get subtle. The paper sets `c1 = c2 = 1e-10`. They look like a numerical hack — and they basically are — but their *size* matters enormously, because they live in the same magnitude region as the actual feature statistics. CLIP-feature means are typically O(0.01–0.1); if you set `c` too large, you swamp the real signal and the ratio collapses toward 1 for every input pair. If you set `c` too small, you risk overflow or division by zero on rare inputs.

The widget below sweeps `c` over twelve orders of magnitude. Watch what happens to the response curve as the constant grows.

<RatioCollapse />

When `c` is at the paper's value of `1e-10` the curve has meaningful variation across the input range. By the time `c` reaches `1e-6` the curve is starting to flatten. By `1e-3` it has collapsed entirely — every input pair gets the same fidelity score. This is what "silent failure mode" looks like: no crash, no warning, no obviously-wrong output. Just a metric that has lost the ability to discriminate, and a downstream caller who has no idea anything is wrong.

This trap bit me once during implementation. I had typed `1e-6` instead of `1e-10` while transcribing the formula, the e2e parity test against the PyIQA reference still passed at low decimal places, and I only caught it when I ran a wider regression sweep across multiple distortion types. The lesson stuck.

## The adapter — where "adaptive" actually lives

The fidelity head and the two naturalness heads each produce a score. The adapter is what blends those three numbers into the final A-FINE output. The blend is parameterised by the two naturalness scores themselves — that is the trick.

Intuitively: if the reference image looks high-quality (high `s_nat_r`), trust the fidelity score. If the reference looks low-quality, the fidelity-to-reference signal is itself unreliable; put more weight on how natural the *distorted* image looks on its own.

The widget below draws the score surface as a function of `s_nat_d` and `s_nat_r`, holding the raw fidelity score `s_fid` fixed at a slider-controlled value. Hover or click cells to compare the score at `(s_nat_d, s_nat_r)` against its mirror at `(s_nat_r, s_nat_d)` — the swap-twin. If those two scores were always identical, the metric would be symmetric and we'd be back to a standard FR-IQA metric. The interesting region is where they differ.

## The CLIP ViT inlining

About half the PR's lines are the CLIP Vision Transformer reimplementation. The reason this is half the PR and not a one-line `use clip_vit::*` is that the Rust ML ecosystem in 2026 does not have a stock CLIP-ViT module that Burn can pull from. We have a choice: depend on an external Rust crate (typically less-maintained, with weight-format mismatches), use Burn's general-purpose transformer module (would need to be heavily parameterised to match CLIP's exact specification), or inline a CLIP-specific implementation.

Inlining won — for the precedent reasons (LPIPS, DISTS, FID all inlined) and because it lets the PR be self-contained for review.

The inlined ViT lives across three files:

- `clip_vit.rs` is the top-level: patch embedding, positional embedding, class token, the stack of twelve transformer blocks, and the final layer-norm.
- `clip_attention.rs` is one transformer block: pre-norm, fused-QKV multi-head self-attention, MLP with QuickGELU.
- `quick_gelu.rs` is the QuickGELU activation function on its own.

Here's a sketch of the block in Rust:

```rust
// crates/burn-train/src/metric/vision/afine/clip_attention.rs (excerpt)

#[derive(Module, Debug)]
pub struct ClipBlock<B: Backend> {
    norm1: LayerNorm<B>,
    attn: MultiHeadAttention<B>,
    norm2: LayerNorm<B>,
    mlp_fc1: Linear<B>,
    mlp_act: QuickGelu,
    mlp_fc2: Linear<B>,
}

impl<B: Backend> ClipBlock<B> {
    pub fn forward(&self, x: Tensor<B, 3>) -> Tensor<B, 3> {
        // Pre-norm transformer block (CLIP's choice, not BERT's).
        let attn_out = self.attn.forward(self.norm1.forward(x.clone()));
        let x = x + attn_out;
        let mlp_in = self.norm2.forward(x.clone());
        let mlp_out = self.mlp_fc2.forward(
            self.mlp_act.forward(self.mlp_fc1.forward(mlp_in))
        );
        x + mlp_out
    }
}
```

The pre-norm placement matters. Some transformers — BERT and the original Vaswani paper — normalise *after* the residual addition. CLIP normalises before. If you get this backwards you'll produce numbers that look plausible but disagree with the reference implementation at every depth.

## The implementation traps

The four interesting bugs I hit while porting the metric.

### Trap 1 — QuickGELU is not GELU

CLIP uses an activation function called QuickGELU, not the standard GELU you'd find in most other transformers. The two are almost the same but not quite.

Standard GELU is defined as `x · Φ(x)` where Φ is the cumulative normal distribution function — implemented in practice via the error function. QuickGELU is `x · σ(1.702 · x)` where σ is the standard sigmoid. The numerical difference is small but real: the peak gap is around 0.014 (about 1.5 % of the function's range), and it lives in the middle of the curve where most activations actually sit.

<GeluComparison />

If you substitute standard GELU for QuickGELU in a CLIP ViT and run a parity test against the reference PyTorch implementation, your outputs will agree to about three decimal places — which looks like a successful port until you start looking at fourth-decimal precision and realise the error is growing slowly with depth. The model "works" but it doesn't *match*. The fix is to implement QuickGELU specifically (it's a one-line function) and use it. That is what `quick_gelu.rs` exists to do.

### Trap 2 — fused-QKV transposed split

The standard transformer textbook has three separate Linear layers for Q, K, and V. CLIP fuses them into a single Linear that projects to 3 × `embed_dim`, then splits the result into the three projections. The fused form is more efficient but introduces a transposition trap.

If the fused projection produces a tensor of shape `[batch, seq, 3 · embed]`, you have two ways to split it back into Q, K, V:

1. **Reshape then split along the head axis:** `view([batch, seq, 3, embed]) → split(dim=2)`. Each of Q, K, V is `[batch, seq, embed]`. The mental model: the channels for Q come first, then K, then V, all interleaved per-head.
2. **Split along the channel axis directly:** `split(3 · embed, dim=-1)`. Each of Q, K, V is `[batch, seq, embed]`. The mental model: the channels are sectioned into three contiguous blocks — first the Q block, then K, then V.

Both produce tensors of the right shape, but they give different *values* unless your fused Linear was specifically trained for one layout. CLIP's PyTorch implementation uses approach (1). I initially implemented approach (2) in Rust and got an output that was numerically wrong but type-correct at every layer of the network. The parity test caught it, but only because I'd seeded the parity test with weights from the reference checkpoint — without that, I'd have shipped a metric that "worked" with random weights and silently disagreed with the paper.

The fix: match CLIP's split convention exactly. The comment on the affected lines now reads `// QKV split must match CLIP's interleaved-per-head layout; do not change to channel-axis split`.

### Trap 3 — 0-D scalar drop

This one was less subtle but more annoying. The naturalness head produces a single scalar per image. In `burn`, a single scalar is a `Tensor<B, 0>` — zero-dimensional. When you stack the two image scores together to feed the adapter, you `cat` them along a new axis 0.

`cat` on `Tensor<B, 0>` is a no-op in some backends — it produces `Tensor<B, 0>` still, because there's no axis to grow. The fix is to `unsqueeze(0)` each scalar to `Tensor<B, 1>` first, then `cat` along axis 0 to produce `Tensor<B, 1>` of length 2. The trap is that this fails silently: the type system accepts the no-op `cat` and the downstream code happens to broadcast in a way that produces a number, just not the right number.

Caught by the parity test. Fixed by always materialising scalars to 1-D before stacking. The pattern now appears in three places across the heads/adapter code.

### Trap 4 — preprocessing inconsistency

The CLIP image preprocessing is bicubic resize to 224×224 followed by per-channel mean-and-std normalisation using CLIP's specific constants (not ImageNet's). The PyIQA reference uses bilinear resize. The difference is tiny — a fraction of a percent in pixel values — but it shifts the metric's output enough to fail strict parity tests.

I initially copied PyIQA's bilinear preprocessing. The fix was to match CLIP's bicubic preprocessing instead, which is what the original A-FINE paper trained against. The PyIQA reference is itself slightly inconsistent with the paper here, which is why the comment in the relevant Rust code says `// match CLIP, not PyIQA — PyIQA's bilinear resize diverges from training-time preprocessing by ~0.3%`.

## The PyTorch weight loader

The A-FINE checkpoint ships as a PyTorch `.pth` file. Loading it into Burn means walking the PyTorch tensor dictionary and assigning each tensor to the correct Burn module field — a key-by-key translation across naming conventions.

`burn-store` is Burn's PyTorch-compatibility crate. It can read `.pth` and `.pt` files into a dictionary of `(key, tensor)` pairs. The work the A-FINE loader does is the *mapping*: PyTorch keys like `backbone.transformer.resblocks.0.attn.in_proj_weight` need to land in Burn's `clip_vit.blocks[0].attn.qkv.weight`.

The mapping is mostly mechanical, but a few of the keys required attention:

| PyTorch key pattern | Notes |
|---|---|
| `attn.in_proj_weight` / `in_proj_bias` | Fused QKV. Single tensor; the loader keeps it fused on the Burn side too, matching the inlined CLIP block. |
| `ln_1` / `ln_2` | LayerNorm names; PyIQA uses `norm1` / `norm2`. The loader handles both via a key-rename pass. |
| `mlp.c_fc` / `mlp.c_proj` | The two MLP Linear layers. CLIP's naming. The loader maps them to `mlp_fc1` and `mlp_fc2`. |
| `class_embedding` | A single 768-dim vector. Loaded into the class-token parameter. |
| `positional_embedding` | Shape `[197, 768]`. Loaded directly into the positional-embedding parameter. |
| Calibrator scalars | Five floats: β₁ through β₅ for the logistic. Stored in the same checkpoint, under a different prefix (`calibrator.*`). |
| Adapter scalars | A handful of weights for the adaptive blend. Same prefix pattern. |

About 200 of `weights.rs`'s 264 lines are the explicit key-to-key mapping. The other 60 lines handle the wrapping (loading the `.pth` file, walking the dictionary, returning a fully-populated `A-Fine` module).

It is the kind of code that nobody enjoys writing — long, mostly-mechanical, error-prone in subtle ways — and the kind that is genuinely valuable once it exists, because every future user of A-FINE-on-Burn loads weights with one function call rather than re-implementing the mapping.

### One honest detail: the codecov gap

The PR's patch coverage was 76.18 %. The project target is 80 %.

<CoverageGauge
  patchPct={76.18}
  targetPct={80}
  note="Codecov flagged the gap. The maintainer accepted the trade-off — the under-tested code is the PyTorch weight loader, which is structurally hard to unit-test without shipping a large checkpoint fixture. End-to-end parity tests cover it instead."

It is worth being honest about this. A perfect engineering writeup would say "merged with full coverage and clean CI". The reality is that the codecov check failed and the PR merged anyway. Why?

The under-tested code is concentrated in `weights.rs` — specifically the long key-to-key mapping that walks the PyTorch dictionary. Unit-testing each branch of that mapping would require either (a) shipping a fixture `.pth` file of meaningful size with the repo, or (b) constructing synthetic PyTorch dictionaries with the right key shapes inside the test, which is most of the way to re-implementing the loader inside the test. Neither was the right call. The end-to-end parity test (does the loaded module produce the same scores as PyIQA's reference Python implementation, given the same checkpoint?) covers `weights.rs` functionally — it just doesn't generate line-coverage credit because the parity test is an integration test, not a unit test.

`laggui` accepted the trade-off without asking for it to be addressed. The PR merged with the codecov warning visible on the page. That is what an honest engineering writeup looks like: the metric got built, the metric got merged, and one of the boxes was not ticked because ticking it wasn't worth what it would have cost.

## What A-FINE doesn't do

For completeness, the limits.

| Concern | A-FINE's behaviour |
|---|---|
| Geometric distortion (rotation, perspective) | Not explicitly modelled. Mild rotations and shifts are handled by the CLIP ViT's inherent translation-ish invariance, but large geometric transformations will collapse the fidelity score. |
| Saliency | Treats all parts of the image as equally important. A face being slightly off in an otherwise-good image won't get extra weight relative to background detail. |
| Temporal consistency | Single-image metric. Says nothing about how a video's frames relate. |
| Adversarial robustness | Not designed for. Inputs crafted to fool the CLIP backbone will fool A-FINE. |
| Out-of-distribution images | Trained on natural photo distributions. Diagrams, line art, paintings, screenshots all sit outside the training manifold and A-FINE's scores on them are less calibrated. |
| Distributional comparison | Per-pair metric. Doesn't compare distributions of images the way FID does. |

A-FINE's pitch isn't that it's the universal IQA metric. Its pitch is that for the family of full-reference IQA tasks where the reference image isn't perfect, it does meaningfully better than older metrics. That is a narrow but useful claim and the metric backs it up on the paper's benchmarks.

## What I'd tell another contributor

The five lessons that generalise outside this specific PR:

1. **Read your precedents before you read the paper.** The four merged perceptual-metric PRs in Burn taught me more about how to ship A-FINE into Burn than the A-FINE paper itself did. The paper tells you what the metric is; the precedents tell you what the codebase wants from your PR.
2. **Long silences are survivable; technical depth at the comeback message is what survives them.** A 25-day silence on a claim looks bad. A 25-day silence followed by a precise message asking the right architectural questions looks like due diligence. The shape of the comeback message is the load-bearing piece.
3. **Maintainer-as-collaborator, not maintainer-as-gatekeeper.** `laggui` corrected my struct shape, said *"your response time was totally fine btw"* when I apologised unnecessarily, and merged a PR with 76.18 % patch coverage because the trade-off made sense. Treating the review as a conversation rather than an audit makes the conversation easier.
4. **One concern per PR — even if bundling feels efficient.** A-FINE shipped as one PR because that matched the precedent and because the inlined-backbone-plus-metric is one concern under the right framing. The next contribution (TensorContainer panic fix) is a strictly different concern and ships as a separate PR even though both touch Burn's gradients code.
5. **Inline, don't depend, when the dependency would be a single-user crate.** The CLIP ViT inside `afine/` is private to A-FINE today. When a second CLIP-based metric lands in Burn, the refactor extracts it. Until then, inlining is the right shape — the alternative would have been an external Rust CLIP crate that nobody else uses and whose maintenance burden falls on this PR.

The work is the point. The merged PR is a deliverable. Doing the work in a way that survives review, matches the project's house style, and leaves the codebase in a better shape than it found is the actual goal.
